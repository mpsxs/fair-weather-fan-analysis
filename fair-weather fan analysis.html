Effect of Win Proportions on Attendance Rates

This report will explore the relationship between the win proportions of several teams (Mariners, Yankees, Red Sox, and Royals) and their corresponding attendance rates over time. I will be using Sean Lahman’s baseball data, specifically teams.csv for my analysis.

Below, I calculate the average attendance per game by dividing the total attendance each year by the number of games in that year. In the interest of comparability, I limit the data to 1970 and later, as the Mariners and Royals data begins there, whereas the Yankees and Red Sox go back as early as 1901. The data has no missing values and so no munging was necessary for this step.

def dataframe_preparation(df):
    #calculates win_ratio column
    df['win_ratio'] = df['W'] / df['G']
    #calculates average_attendance column
    df['average_attendance'] = df['attendance'] / df['G'] 
    #shifts average_attendance column 1 year
    df['shifted_attendance'] = df['average_attendance'].shift()
    #removes most recent year (shift causes NaN in attendance)
    df = df[df.shifted_attendance.isnull() == False]
    #limits data to after 1970 (for Yankees and Red Sox)
    df = df[df['yearID'] > 1970]
    return df

The distribution of attendance differs between each team. The Redsox are skewed left, the Mariners are skewed right, the Yankees are bimodal, and the Royals are roughly normal with a exceptional bin at 2.1-2.2 million. Additionally, there appears to be initial evidence for a correlation between win proportion and attendance. The yankees, with the highest average win proportion also have the highest average attendance. The Redsox follow with the second highest average in both. This trend does not continue with the Mariners and Royals. The Royals have the 3rd highest win proportion and the lowest average attendance. The Mariners have the lowest win proportion rate but have the 3rd highest attendance. I will explore the relationship between these variables further.

def generate_plot_with_regression_sameyear(df, color, teamname):
    #generates regression function
    fit = np.polyfit(df['win_ratio'], df['shifted_attendance'], 1)
    fit_fn = np.poly1d(fit)

    plt.figure(1)
    plt.plot(df['win_ratio'], df['shifted_attendance'], color + 'o', df['win_ratio'], fit_fn(df['win_ratio']), 'k-')
    plt.xlabel('Win proportion')
    plt.ylabel('Attendance the following year')
    plt.title(teamname)
    plt.show()

Conclusion

Similar to the same-year analysis, the statistics here show a correlation, albeit weaker, for the Mariners and Yankees. The Royals do not maintain this correlation. This suggests some interesting things. For the Mariners and Yankees, it appears that win rate affects attendance not only the same year but also of the next. The Royals are affected on a same-year basis but performance doesn't seem to carry over between seasons. Attendance for the Redsox is not correlated to win proportions.

What does this mean?

There is a difference in the way win rates affect attendance between different teams. On the assumption that every sports team has some committed fanbase that will attend games regardless of performance, it follows that the difference between teams as described above can be attributed in part to “fair-weather fans”, namely those who attend games only while the team is performing well. Thus from the data I can loosely infer that the stronger the relationship between win rates and attendance, the greater number of fair-weather fans a team has. In the cases considered here it would appear that the Mariners have the greatest number of these fans, whereas the Red Sox have the least.

Statistical considerations –

There are a number of variables that may also affect attendance rates aside from fair-weather fans. In the plots of yearly attendance dating before 1970, there were significant bumps in attendance following the end of World War II. The steroid era of baseball, from around 1991 to 2003 also appear to have generated a bump in attendance as well. Declining attendance in very recent years may be due to the economic recession. In short, attendance is likely to be affected by a large number of different variables. The above analysis must be considered with this in mind.
